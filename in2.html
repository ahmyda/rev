üìö 1. Core Definitions (Lecture 1)

Intercultural Communication

‚ñº

Definition: The exchange of information between individuals or groups from different cultural backgrounds.

Key Points:

  • It's both a process and a skill (Samovar, Porter, & McDaniel, 2015)
  • Involves understanding how culture affects communication
  • Essential in today's globalized world
Example:

An Algerian teacher explaining a concept to a Chinese student must consider different learning styles, communication norms, and cultural references.

Culture

‚ñº

Traditional View: A shared system of meanings, values, and practices that shape perception Hall, 1976.

Modern View (Lecture 3): Culture is fluid, hybrid, and constantly changing.

  • Not tied to nations only
  • Includes everyday practices, beliefs, shifting identities
  • Influenced by globalization
Example from Algerian Context:

A student might speak formal Arabic in class, use Darija with friends, and simplified English online with international peers. This shows multiple cultural identities.

Interculturality

‚ñº

Beyond simple contact: Involves questioning assumptions and analyzing power relations Byram, 1997; Kramsch, 1998.

Key Aspects:

  • Deconstructing stereotypes
  • Understanding negotiated identities
  • Analyzing who dominates communication
Example:

When Algerian and French colleagues work together, whose meeting style dominates? Who has to adapt more? These power dynamics are part of interculturality.

üß© 2. Key Components of Intercultural Communication

1. Culture as Meaning System

‚ñº

Culture shapes how we see the world. It's not just traditions, but our mental programming.

In communication: Culture affects what we say, how we say it, and how we interpret others.

2. Communication Process

‚ñº

Symbolic process of sharing meaning through:

  • Verbal messages (words)
  • Non-verbal messages (gestures, tone, silence)

Cross-culturally: Same behavior can have different meanings in different cultures.

Example:

Eye contact = confidence (Western) vs disrespect (some East Asian cultures). Silence = disagreement (some cultures) vs respect/thinking (others).

3. Context

‚ñº

The setting where communication happens, including:

  • Situational context (where, when)
  • Relational context (who's speaking to whom)
  • Cultural context (cultural norms)

4. Intercultural Competence

‚ñº

Definition: Ability to interact effectively and appropriately with people from different cultures Deardorff, 2006.

Includes:

  • Knowledge of cultures
  • Skills to adapt communication
  • Attitudes of openness and respect
  • Internal outcomes (flexible thinking)
  • External outcomes (effective behavior)

üéì 3. Theoretical Perspectives

Major Theories (from Lectures 1 & 2)

Hall's High- vs Low-Context Cultures 1976

‚ñº

High-Context Cultures: Rely on implicit messages, body language, shared knowledge.

  • Examples: Japan, Arab countries, Algeria
  • Communication: Indirect, polite, relationship-focused
  • "Read between the lines"

Low-Context Cultures: Prefer direct, explicit verbal messages.

  • Examples: USA, Germany, Switzerland
  • Communication: Direct, clear, task-focused
  • "Say what you mean"

Hofstede's 6 Cultural Dimensions 2001

‚ñº

Six value dimensions that explain cultural differences:

1. Power Distance (PDI)

Acceptance of hierarchy vs preference for equality.

2. Individualism vs Collectivism (IDV)

"I" vs "We" orientation.

3. Masculinity vs Femininity (MAS)

Competition vs care orientation.

4. Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)

Need for rules vs comfort with ambiguity.

5. Long-term vs Short-term Orientation (LTO)

Future focus vs tradition focus.

6. Indulgence vs Restraint (IVR)

Allowance for enjoyment vs discipline.

Gudykunst's Anxiety/Uncertainty Management 2005

‚ñº

Focuses on managing the anxiety and uncertainty we feel in intercultural encounters.

Key idea: Effective communication requires managing these feelings, not eliminating them.

Appadurai's Global Cultural Flows (Scapes) 1996

‚ñº

Globalization as 5 interconnected flows:

  • Ethnoscapes: Movement of people
  • Mediascapes: Flow of media/images
  • Technoscapes: Spread of technology
  • Financescapes: Movement of money
  • Ideoscapes: Flow of ideas/ideologies

Key idea: Cultures are constantly remixed through these global flows.

üè´ 4. Intercultural Communication in Didactics

Why It Matters for Teachers

‚ñº

Essential for creating inclusive, culturally responsive learning environments.

Benefits:

  • Promotes mutual respect among diverse students
  • Reduces prejudice and stereotyping
  • Improves classroom interaction
  • Prepares students for global citizenship

Byram's Intercultural Communicative Competence (ICC) 1997

‚ñº

A model that transformed language teaching:

Five Savoirs (Knowledges):

  1. Savoir √™tre: Attitudes (openness, curiosity)
  2. Savoir apprendre: Skills of discovery
  3. Savoirs: Knowledge about cultures
  4. Savoir faire: Skills to interact
  5. Savoir s'engager: Critical cultural awareness

Impact: Shifted focus from just language accuracy to cultural understanding and mediation.

From Language Teaching to Global Competence

‚ñº

Traditional approach: Teach language + facts about target culture.

Modern approach: Develop students' ability to:

  • Navigate between cultures
  • Mediate misunderstandings
  • Think critically about cultural issues
  • Participate in global communities

üåç 5. Global Perspective (Lecture 2)

Globalization's Impact

‚ñº

Globalization = Increasing interconnectedness worldwide.

Effects on communication:

  • More frequent cross-cultural contact
  • Virtual exchanges without physical travel
  • Cultural hybridization (mixing of cultures)
  • Digital communication as routine

English as Global Lingua Franca

‚ñº

English as the dominant international language Crystal, 2003.

Positive: Facilitates global understanding.

Challenges:

  • Cultural/linguistic dominance
  • Marginalization of smaller languages
  • Need for intercultural awareness WITH language skills

Digital Intercultural Communication

‚ñº

Virtual environments add complexity:

  • Missing non-verbal cues (body language, tone)
  • Higher risk of misunderstandings
  • Need for extra clarity and empathy
Example:

A sarcastic comment in a text message can be misinterpreted without vocal tone or facial expressions to show it's a joke.

Challenges in Global Communication

‚ñº

Stereotypes & Prejudice

Oversimplified beliefs about groups that create barriers.

Ethnocentrism

Believing one's own culture is superior, judging others by own norms.

Culture Shock (4 Stages)

  1. Honeymoon: Excitement
  2. Frustration: Anxiety
  3. Adjustment: Adaptation
  4. Adaptation: Bicultural competence

üí¨ 6. The Intercultural Speaker (Lecture 3)

From "Native Speaker" to "Intercultural Speaker"

‚ñº

Old ideal: Sound like a native speaker.

New ideal: Become an intercultural speaker who can:

  • Mediate between cultures
  • Explain cultural differences
  • Navigate "in-between" spaces
  • Adapt communication style

The "Inter" Space Concept

‚ñº

The crucial zone where cultures meet and interact Kramsch, 2009.

Characteristics:

  • Communication is co-constructed here
  • Identities are negotiated
  • Misunderstandings become learning opportunities
  • Meaning is created together
Example:

An Algerian freelancer and German client don't use purely Algerian or German communication. They create a hybrid style specific to their working relationship.

Three Meanings of "Intercultural"

‚ñº

1. Intercultural Competence

Developing attitudes of curiosity, empathy, openness.

2. Intercultural Communication

Interacting across cultural boundaries using language skills.

3. Intercultural Citizenship

Using language to participate ethically in global communities.

Deconstructing Cultural Assumptions

‚ñº

Critical process in intercultural education:

  1. Question stereotypes: "Do all British people really..."
  2. Observe real interactions: See how people actually communicate
  3. Analyze power relations: Whose norms dominate?

üé≠ 7. Practical Scenarios & Analysis

10 common intercultural situations with key concept analysis

1Meeting Style Clash

German manager wants quick, agenda-focused meetings. Algerian team expects relationship-building first.

Key Concepts:

Low-Context vs High-Context + Task vs Relationship orientation

2Silence in Classroom

Chinese student never speaks unless called on. American teacher thinks she's not participating.

Key Concepts:

High Power Distance + Different views of participation

3Email Misunderstanding

Direct Dutch email offends indirect Japanese receiver who expects polite framing.

Key Concepts:

Direct vs Indirect communication styles

4Deadline Conflict

Spanish team misses deadline for family event. German partner sees as unprofessional.

Key Concepts:

Monochronic vs Polychronic time + Individualism vs Collectivism

5Virtual Team Issues

On Zoom, team members from high-context cultures struggle without non-verbal cues.

Key Concepts:

Digital communication challenges + High-context reliance

6Feedback Style

American gives direct criticism. Algerian recipient feels publicly shamed.

Key Concepts:

Face-saving + Public vs private feedback norms

7Group Work Conflict

Individualist students want separate tasks. Collectivist students want consensus on everything.

Key Concepts:

Individualism vs Collectivism in teamwork

8Cultural Adaptation

Algerian student in France goes through all 4 stages of culture shock.

Key Concepts:

Culture Shock stages + Adaptation process

9Language Power Dynamics

Meeting in English gives advantage to native speakers, marginalizes others.

Key Concepts:

English as Lingua Franca + Power relations

10Hybrid Identity

French-Algerian student creates own communication style blending both cultures.

Key Concepts:

Intercultural Speaker + Third Space creation
