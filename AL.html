Lecture 01: Introducing Applied Linguistics

Definition of Applied Linguistics

Applied Linguistics is an interdisciplinary field of inquiry that addresses language-related issues to understand their roles in individuals' lives and societal conditions. It draws on various theoretical and methodological approaches from humanities to social and natural sciences to solve real-world language problems.

Historical Development

Applied Linguistics emerged after World War II with the expansion of language teaching, particularly English. Initially focused on filling knowledge gaps for language teachers, it has since expanded to address a wide range of language-related issues in various domains.

Evolution of Applied Linguistics
  • 1950s-1960s: Focus on language teaching and contrastive analysis
  • 1970s: Expansion to language acquisition, error analysis, and sociolinguistics
  • 1980s: Inclusion of discourse analysis, pragmatics, and language testing
  • 1990s-present: Broad interdisciplinary scope including computational linguistics, forensic linguistics, language policy, and more

Applied Linguistics vs. Linguistics Applied

Applied Linguistics

  • Multidisciplinary approach drawing on psychology, sociology, education, etc.
  • Views language problems as requiring multiple perspectives
  • Mediation between theory and practice
  • Recognizes linguistic insights require interpretation in context
  • Seeks to understand how perception of problems might be changed

Linguistics Applied

  • Direct application of linguistic concepts to problems
  • Assumes language problems have primarily linguistic solutions
  • Unilateral application of linguistic theories
  • Views linguistic insights as self-evident
  • Focuses on matching linguistic findings to pre-existing problems

Scope of Applied Linguistics

Domain Subfields Applications
Language Education L1 education, L2/FL education, literacy, curriculum design Classroom teaching, materials development, teacher training
Clinical Linguistics Speech pathology, language disorders, aphasia therapy Diagnosis, intervention, rehabilitation for communication disorders
Language Testing & Assessment Proficiency testing, achievement testing, diagnostic assessment TOEFL, IELTS, placement tests, classroom assessment
Forensic Linguistics Authorship analysis, discourse analysis in legal contexts Courtroom discourse, plagiarism detection, threat assessment
Translation & Interpretation Literary translation, conference interpreting, localization Cross-cultural communication, international diplomacy, publishing
Language Policy & Planning Language rights, language revitalization, official language policy Government policy, educational planning, minority language protection

Fundamental Questions in the Field

  • Educational Questions: What language skills should be taught? Should dialect speakers be encouraged toward standard forms? Which languages should be used in multilingual schools?
  • Clinical Questions: Should deaf children learn sign language or focus on oral communication? How can speech disorders be effectively diagnosed and treated?
  • Sociolinguistic Questions: Should language change be controlled? Should dying languages be preserved? Is the spread of English as a lingua franca positive or negative?
  • Political Questions: Is language being used for political oppression? Which languages should have official status? How can language rights be protected?
  • Technological Questions: Can translation be effectively done by computer? How can technology enhance language learning? What are the implications of AI for language use?

Lecture 02: Contrastive Analysis (CA)

Contrastive Analysis Definition

The systematic study of a pair of languages to identify structural differences and similarities, with the goal of predicting learning difficulties in second language acquisition. Developed by Robert Lado in the 1950s based on behaviorist theories of language learning as habit formation.

Theoretical Foundations

CA is grounded in behaviorist psychology and structural linguistics. Key assumptions include:

  • Language learning involves forming new habits
  • L1 habits interfere with L2 learning (negative transfer)
  • Similar structures facilitate learning (positive transfer)
  • Learning difficulty correlates with linguistic difference
  • Errors result primarily from L1 interference

Arabic-English Contrastive Examples

Phonological Level: Arabic lacks /p/, /v/, /ŋ/, /ʒ/ sounds, leading to substitutions like /b/ for /p/, /f/ for /v/.

Morphological Level: Arabic lacks third person singular '-s' marker, leading to errors like *"he go" instead of "he goes".

Syntactic Level: Arabic has VSO word order in some contexts, leading to errors like *"Speaks he English?"

Semantic Level: Arabic "yad" corresponds to both "hand" and "arm" in English, leading to potential confusion.

CA Methodology

  1. Selection: Choose comparable language elements for analysis
  2. Description: Describe both languages using the same linguistic framework
  3. Comparison: Identify points of similarity and difference
  4. Prediction: Forecast areas of difficulty based on differences
  5. Verification: Test predictions against learner data (in modern CA)
  6. Application: Develop teaching materials addressing predicted difficulties

Major Criticisms of Contrastive Analysis

Theoretical Criticisms

  • Behaviorist foundation: Based on outdated habit formation theory, ignoring cognitive processes
  • Deterministic view: Assumes L1 determines L2 acquisition path, neglecting learner agency
  • Static comparison: Compares idealized language systems, not actual learner language
  • Neglect of developmental factors: Doesn't account for acquisition sequences or stages

Empirical Criticisms

  • Overprediction: Many predicted difficulties don't materialize (e.g., English /tʃ/ for Arab learners)
  • Underprediction: Many actual errors aren't predicted (e.g., overgeneralizations like *"goed")
  • False equivalence: Difficulty establishing what constitutes "same" or "different" across languages
  • Homogeneous assumption: Assumes all learners with same L1 have same difficulties
Modern Perspective on CA

While classical CA has been largely superseded, modern approaches recognize the value of selective contrastive analysis. Contemporary researchers acknowledge L1 influence but view it as one factor among many in SLA. CA insights remain valuable for:

  • Understanding specific pronunciation difficulties
  • Designing materials for homogeneous learner groups
  • Raising teacher awareness of potential L1 interference
  • Informing error correction strategies

Lecture 03: Error Analysis (EA)

Error Analysis Definition

A technique for identifying, describing, and explaining systematic deviations in learner language. Emerged in the late 1960s as a response to CA limitations, shifting focus from teaching to learning and viewing errors as evidence of the acquisition process rather than failure.

Theoretical Shift: From CA to EA

Contrastive Analysis (1950s-60s)

  • Teacher-centered: Focus on what to teach
  • A priori: Predicts errors before they occur
  • Linguistic: Based on language comparison
  • Behaviorist: Errors as bad habits to eliminate
  • Interference: Errors primarily from L1 transfer

Error Analysis (1970s)

  • Learner-centered: Focus on how learning occurs
  • A posteriori: Analyzes actual learner errors
  • Psycholinguistic: Based on learner language data
  • Cognitive: Errors as evidence of learning processes
  • Multiple sources: Errors from various cognitive processes

Errors vs. Mistakes

Aspect Errors Mistakes
Definition Systematic deviations reflecting current competence Random performance slips due to fatigue, inattention, etc.
Source Incomplete knowledge of L2 rules Failure to apply known rules correctly
Consistency Consistent across similar contexts Inconsistent, sporadic occurrence
Self-correction Cannot be self-corrected Can be self-corrected when pointed out
Significance Reveals developmental stage, valuable for research Reveals performance factors, less significant for research

Classification of Errors

Error Classification Framework

Interlingual Errors
(Transfer from L1)
• Phonological (e.g., /b/ for /p/)
• Morphological (e.g., *"book Ahmad")
• Syntactic (e.g., *"Huda student")
• Semantic (e.g., *"close the TV")
Intralingual Errors
(Within L2)
• Overgeneralization (e.g., *"goed")
• Simplification (e.g., *"I no like")
• Incomplete rule application
• False concepts hypothesized
Other Sources
• Communication strategies
• Teacher-induced errors
• Materials-induced errors
• Learning context factors

EA Methodology (Corder, 1974)

  1. Collection: Gather samples of learner language (written/oral, natural/elicited)
  2. Identification: Spot deviations from target language norms
  3. Description: Classify errors linguistically (phonological, syntactic, etc.)
  4. Explanation: Determine probable causes (interlingual, intralingual, etc.)
  5. Evaluation: Assess error gravity (global vs. local errors)
  6. Remediation: Develop pedagogical responses based on analysis
Example Error Analysis: Arab Learners of English

Error: *"Yesterday I go to market."

Analysis:

  • Type: Tense error (present instead of past)
  • Classification: Intralingual (developmental)
  • Probable Cause: Overgeneralization of present tense for past time reference with temporal adverb
  • Explanation: Learner has not yet acquired past tense marking but uses "yesterday" to indicate past time
  • Gravity: Local error (doesn't impede communication significantly)
  • Remediation: Focused practice on past tense forms with temporal adverbs

Major Criticisms of Error Analysis

Methodological Issues

  • Avoidance: Learners may avoid structures they find difficult
  • Error identification: Difficult to distinguish error types and sources
  • Norm dependency: Relies on questionable "native speaker" norms
  • Data collection: Cross-sectional data provides snapshots, not developmental paths

Theoretical Issues

  • Focus on errors: Neglects what learners do correctly
  • Product-oriented: Analyzes output, not learning processes
  • Static view: Doesn't show progression in acquisition
  • Overemphasis on form: Neglects communicative success
EA's Lasting Contributions

Despite criticisms, EA made significant contributions to SLA research:

  • Shifted focus from teaching to learning processes
  • Established that many errors are developmental, not from L1 interference
  • Highlighted learners as active hypothesis-testers
  • Provided methodology for analyzing learner language
  • Paved way for interlanguage theory and performance analysis

Lecture 04: Interlanguage Theory

Interlanguage Definition

A separate linguistic system developed by L2 learners that exists between their L1 and the target L2. Term coined by Larry Selinker in 1972. It is systematic, dynamic, rule-governed, and influenced by multiple cognitive and social factors. Also called "approximative system" (Nemser) or "transitional competence" (Corder).

Characteristics of Interlanguage

Systematic

Governed by internal rules that can be described linguistically. Learners don't produce random errors but follow predictable patterns. For example, a learner might consistently use SVO word order even when producing errors within that framework.

Dynamic

Constantly evolving as learners receive more input and restructure their mental representations. Interlanguage is not static but progresses through developmental sequences, often resembling stages of L1 acquisition.

Permeable

Open to influence from both L1 and L2, allowing incorporation of new rules and restructuring of existing ones. Rules are not fixed but can be revised based on new input or feedback.

Variable

May show different forms in different contexts (e.g., careful vs. casual speech, written vs. oral production). This systematic variation reflects the influence of social and psychological factors.

Fossilization

Fossilization: Definition and Causes

Fossilization refers to the permanent retention of non-target features in interlanguage, resistant to further instruction or exposure. Selinker estimated only about 5% of learners achieve native-like competence in all aspects of an L2.

Causes of Fossilization

  • Age: Critical period hypothesis suggests adults rarely achieve native-like pronunciation
  • Lack of corrective feedback: Learners may not receive or incorporate corrections
  • Communication satisfaction: When communicative needs are met, motivation to improve decreases
  • L1 entrenchment: Strong neural pathways for L1 structures resist restructuring
  • Identity factors: Some learners may wish to maintain a foreign accent as part of identity
  • Limited input: Insufficient quality or quantity of L2 exposure
Interlanguage Example: Developmental Sequence

Target Structure: English negation

Interlanguage Developmental Stages:

  1. Stage 1: External negation (*"No you are playing")
  2. Stage 2: Internal, pre-verbal negation (*"I no can play")
  3. Stage 3: Auxiliary + negation (*"I can't play")
  4. Stage 4: Target-like negation ("I cannot play")

This sequence is remarkably consistent across learners regardless of L1, suggesting universal developmental processes in SLA.

Interlanguage Methodology

Studying interlanguage requires:

  • Longitudinal data: Tracking learner development over time
  • Multiple data sources: Naturalistic and elicited production
  • Comparison points: L1 baseline, interlanguage samples, target L2 norms
  • Contextual factors: Considering social, psychological, and instructional influences

Criticisms of Interlanguage Theory

  • Definitional vagueness: Unclear boundaries between interlanguage, L1, and L2
  • Methodological challenges: Difficult to obtain longitudinal data and identify systematicity
  • Overemphasis on cognitive factors: Neglects social and affective dimensions
  • Homogenizing tendency: May overlook individual differences in learning paths

Modern Developments

Complexity Theory

Views interlanguage as a complex adaptive system that self-organizes through interaction. Emphasizes non-linear development, variability, and emergence of patterns.

Sociocultural Theory

Emphasizes social interaction and mediation in interlanguage development. Views learning as socially constructed through interaction with more knowledgeable others.

Lecture 05: Language Learning Strategies

Learning Strategies Definition

Conscious techniques, behaviors, or actions that learners employ to enhance their language learning. They are tools for active, self-directed involvement essential for developing communicative competence (Oxford, 1990). Strategies can be taught, learned, and modified based on effectiveness and context.

Historical Development of Strategy Research

  • 1970s: Early studies identifying "good language learner" characteristics
  • 1980s: Development of classification systems (O'Malley & Chamot, Rubin)
  • 1990s: Oxford's comprehensive taxonomy and strategy inventory (SILL)
  • 2000s: Focus on strategy instruction, cultural variations, and individual differences
  • 2010s-present: Integration with neuroscience, technology, and self-regulation theories

Oxford's Strategy Classification System (1990)

Strategy Type Purpose Examples Key Researchers
Memory Strategies Remembering and retrieving information Grouping, imagery, keyword method, structured reviewing Atkinson (1975), Oxford (1990)
Cognitive Strategies Understanding and producing language Practice, analysis, reasoning, summarizing, note-taking O'Malley & Chamot (1990), Wenden (1991)
Compensation Strategies Compensating for missing knowledge Guessing, synonyms, gestures, circumlocution Tarone (1977), Faerch & Kasper (1983)
Metacognitive Strategies Planning and evaluating learning Goal-setting, self-monitoring, self-evaluation, planning Flavell (1979), Wenden (1991)
Affective Strategies Managing emotions and motivation Self-encouragement, anxiety reduction, self-reward Krashen (1982), Arnold (1999)
Social Strategies Learning with others Asking questions, cooperating, cultural awareness Vygotsky (1978), Oxford (1990)

Factors Influencing Strategy Choice

Individual Factors

  • Age: Children use task-specific strategies; adults use generalized strategies
  • Motivation: Highly motivated learners use more strategies
  • Personality: Extroverts use more social/communication strategies
  • Learning Style: Visual, auditory, kinesthetic preferences

Situational Factors

  • Task Type: Different tasks elicit different strategies
  • Learning Context: Classroom vs. naturalistic settings
  • Cultural Background: Educational traditions influence strategy use
  • Language Being Learned: Some strategies work better for certain languages

Strategy Instruction Research

Effective Strategy Instruction (Chamot, 2005)

Research shows strategy instruction is most effective when it follows these principles:

  1. Explicit: Clearly explain and demonstrate strategies
  2. Integrated: Embed strategy instruction in regular language activities
  3. Contextualized: Teach strategies in authentic language tasks
  4. Personalized: Help learners identify strategies that work for them
  5. Gradual: Introduce strategies progressively over time
  6. Evaluative: Teach learners to assess strategy effectiveness
Characteristics of Good Language Learners (Naiman et al., 1978)
  1. Find their own way: Take responsibility for learning and develop personal approaches
  2. Organize information: Create systems for remembering and retrieving language
  3. Are creative: Experiment with language and make intelligent guesses
  4. Create practice opportunities: Seek out and create chances to use the language
  5. Learn to live with uncertainty: Tolerate ambiguity and make progress despite gaps
  6. Use memory strategies: Employ techniques to recall what's been learned
  7. Monitor their language: Develop critical sensitivity to language use
  8. Use contextual cues: Infer meaning from situation and context

Cultural Variations in Strategy Use

Research shows significant cultural differences in strategy preferences:

  • Asian learners: Often prefer memorization, repetition, and formal practice strategies
  • Western learners: Tend to use more communication and social strategies
  • Middle Eastern learners: Often combine memorization with social interaction strategies
  • Individualist vs. collectivist cultures: Show different preferences for independent vs. cooperative strategies

Criticisms of Strategy Research

  • Definitional problems: Unclear boundaries between strategies, styles, and techniques
  • Measurement issues: Self-report data may not reflect actual strategy use
  • Contextual oversimplification: May neglect how strategies interact with context
  • Causality assumptions: Difficult to establish if strategies cause success or vice versa

Future Research Directions

Neuroscientific Approaches

Using fMRI and EEG to study brain activity during strategy use, examining neural correlates of effective language learning strategies.

Technology-Enhanced Strategies

Investigating how digital tools and online environments create new strategy possibilities and requirements for language learners.

The Evolution of Applied Linguistics: From Structuralism to Social Turn
Approx. 1400 words

Essay Question

Trace the historical development of Applied Linguistics from its origins in structural linguistics and behaviorism to contemporary social and critical approaches. How has the field's understanding of language, learning, and teaching evolved, and what implications does this evolution have for current research and practice?

Key Points to Address

  1. Structuralist foundations: The influence of Saussurean linguistics and Bloomfieldian structuralism on early Applied Linguistics, particularly in Contrastive Analysis.
  2. Behaviorist psychology: How Skinner's operant conditioning and habit formation theories shaped early views of language learning and teaching.
  3. Cognitive revolution: The shift from behaviorism to cognitivism in the 1970s, exemplified by the transition from CA to Error Analysis and Interlanguage theory.
  4. Communicative approaches: The emergence of Communicative Language Teaching in the 1980s and its focus on meaning, function, and authentic communication.
  5. Social turn: The incorporation of sociocultural theory, critical discourse analysis, and identity perspectives in the 1990s and beyond.
  6. Current trends: The role of technology, globalization, and interdisciplinary approaches in contemporary Applied Linguistics.
  7. Implications: How this evolution has changed language teaching methodologies, research priorities, and understandings of language learning success.

Theoretical Integration

  • Connect CA's structuralist foundations to behaviorist psychology
  • Explain how EA and Interlanguage theory reflect cognitive approaches
  • Show how strategy research bridges cognitive and affective dimensions
  • Discuss how social approaches critique earlier individual-cognitive models

Error Analysis and Interlanguage: Revolutionizing Our Understanding of SLA
Approx. 1300 words

Essay Question

How did Error Analysis and Interlanguage theory fundamentally change our understanding of second language acquisition? Compare and contrast these approaches with Contrastive Analysis, and evaluate their contributions to both theory and practice in Applied Linguistics.

Key Points to Address

  1. CA's limitations: Critically analyze the theoretical and empirical shortcomings of Contrastive Analysis that led to the development of EA.
  2. EA's contributions: Examine how Error Analysis shifted focus from teaching to learning, from prediction to description, and from L1 interference to multiple error sources.
  3. Interlanguage breakthrough: Analyze how Interlanguage theory conceptualized learner language as a systematic, evolving system with its own internal logic.
  4. Methodological innovations: Compare the research methodologies of CA, EA, and Interlanguage studies, highlighting their respective strengths and limitations.
  5. Fossilization concept: Evaluate Selinker's concept of fossilization and its implications for understanding ultimate attainment in SLA.
  6. Practical applications: Assess how these theories have influenced language teaching practices, error correction, and curriculum design.
  7. Contemporary relevance: Determine which insights from EA and Interlanguage theory remain relevant in current SLA research and practice.

Theoretical Integration

  • Contrast behaviorist foundations of CA with cognitive foundations of EA and IL
  • Analyze how each approach conceptualizes the learner: passive habit-former vs. active hypothesis-tester
  • Connect IL theory to universal grammar and natural language acquisition research
  • Link strategy research to IL development processes

Approx. 1600 words

Essay Question

Applied Linguistics has expanded far beyond its initial focus on language teaching. Analyze three non-educational domains where Applied Linguistics makes significant contributions (e.g., forensic linguistics, clinical linguistics, language policy). What theoretical frameworks and methodologies are employed in these domains, and how do they exemplify the interdisciplinary nature of Applied Linguistics?

Key Points to Address

  1. Historical expansion: Trace how Applied Linguistics broadened from language teaching to diverse real-world language problems.
  2. Forensic linguistics: Analyze how linguistic analysis contributes to legal contexts (authorship attribution, discourse analysis in courtrooms, plagiarism detection).
  3. Clinical linguistics: Examine the role of linguistic theory in diagnosing and treating language disorders (aphasia, specific language impairment, autism spectrum disorders).
  4. Language policy and planning: Evaluate how Applied Linguistics informs decisions about official languages, language education policies, and language rights.
  5. Methodological diversity: Compare the research methods used in these domains (e.g., corpus analysis in forensic linguistics, experimental designs in clinical linguistics, ethnographic approaches in language policy).
  6. Interdisciplinary connections: Identify how these domains connect linguistics with law, medicine, education, political science, and sociology.
  7. Ethical considerations: Discuss ethical challenges in applying linguistic knowledge to real-world problems (e.g., privacy in forensic analysis, informed consent in clinical settings).
  8. Future directions: Speculate on emerging applications of Applied Linguistics in areas like computational linguistics, neurolinguistics, and environmental communication.

Theoretical Integration

  • Connect forensic linguistics to discourse analysis and pragmatics
  • Link clinical linguistics to psycholinguistics and language acquisition theories
  • Relate language policy to sociolinguistics and critical discourse analysis
  • Show how all domains exemplify Applied Linguistics as "mediation between theory and practice"

Integrating Approaches: A Holistic Model for Understanding Second Language Acquisition
Approx. 1700 words

Essay Question

Synthesize insights from Contrastive Analysis, Error Analysis, Interlanguage theory, and learning strategy research to develop a comprehensive, integrated model of second language acquisition. How can these different perspectives be combined to provide a more complete understanding of the complex processes involved in learning an additional language?

Key Points to Address

  1. Theoretical synthesis: Identify complementary insights from each approach and integrate them into a coherent framework.
  2. L1 role re-evaluated: Reconcile CA's focus on L1 influence with EA/IL's evidence of universal developmental sequences.
  3. Error reinterpretation: Develop a multi-dimensional error taxonomy that incorporates insights from all approaches.
  4. Dynamic development model: Create a model that shows how interlanguage develops through interaction of L1 transfer, universal processes, instruction, and individual strategies.
  5. Individual differences: Integrate strategy research with cognitive, affective, and social dimensions of SLA.
  6. Contextual factors: Incorporate the role of learning environment, instructional methods, and social context into the integrated model.
  7. Pedagogical implications: Derive teaching principles from the integrated model that address multiple aspects of language learning.
  8. Model evaluation: Critically assess the strengths and limitations of your integrated model compared to existing SLA theories.
  9. Research directions: Propose research questions and methodologies that could test and refine the integrated model.

Theoretical Integration

  • Synthesize behaviorist, cognitivist, and social perspectives on SLA
  • Integrate linguistic, psychological, and educational dimensions
  • Balance universal patterns with individual variation
  • Connect micro-level cognitive processes with macro-level social contexts
